How Prediction Markets Work

Trading on events is straightforward. A share's price reflects the market's belief in an outcome's probability, ranging from $0.01 to $0.99.

1. Event Definition

An event with a clear, verifiable outcome is listed. For example: "Will Apple release an AI-powered iPhone by June 2025?"

2. Trading Contracts

You buy shares for the outcome you believe in. A "Yes" share pays out $1 if the event happens, $0 if not. "No" shares are the opposite.

3. Price = Probability

A "Yes" share price of $0.22 implies a 22% chance of the event occurring. As sentiment changes, so does the price, creating trading opportunities.

4. Settlement

Once the event's outcome is known, correct shares pay out $1 each. Your profit is the payout minus your purchase price.

Tech Forecast

"Will SpaceX land Starship on Mars by 2030?"

A trader believes the market is underestimating SpaceX. She buys 1,000 "Yes" shares at $0.22 each, for a total investment of $220.

Months later, after a successful test flight, market confidence grows and the price for "Yes" shares rises to $0.60. She sells her shares for a profit before the event even concludes.

Profit: ($0.60 - $0.22) x 1,000 = $380

Economic Analysis

"Will India's annual GDP growth exceed 7% in 2025?"

An economist is skeptical about optimistic forecasts. He sees the market price for "Yes" shares at $0.85, which he believes is too high. He buys 500 "No" shares at $0.15.

Official results are released, and GDP growth is 6.8%. The "No" outcome is confirmed. His "No" shares settle at $1.00 each.

Profit: (500 x $1.00) - (500 x $0.15) = $425
